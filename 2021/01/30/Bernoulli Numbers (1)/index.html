Let us step back from the exponential generating function to take a closer look at the Bernoulli numbers themselves. When you look at the first terms of the Bernoulli numbers, what do you notice?

Likely, a few of the patterns you see are among the following:

  • $B_n$ is rational.
  • $B_{2n+1}=0$ for $n\geqslant1$ .
  • $B_{2n}$ alternates sign: $B_{4n}<0$ and $B_{4n+2}>0$ for $n\geqslant 1$ .
  • The magnitude of $B_{2n}$ grows very quickly.

We consider each of the possible prime factors of the integers below $N$. There is one-half probability that each of the integers are even (or nearly one-half, depending on the value of $N$), so the probability that they both are even is $\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac12=\dfrac14$. Thus, the probability that the integers do not share $2$ as a factor is exactly $\left(1-\dfrac14\right)$.

Applying similar logic convinces us that the probability that the integers do not share a factor of $3$ is $\left(1-\dfrac19\right)$, that they do not share a factor of $4$ is $\left(1-\dfrac{1}{16}\right)$, and so on, so that the probability that any prime $p$ is not a common factor is $\left(1-\dfrac{1}{p^2}\right)$.
