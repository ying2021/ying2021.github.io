These two observations are, remarkably, true in general. In fact, they give us the following inductive definition for the Bernoulli numbers.

A polynomial, $B_k(y)$, is a Bernoulli polynomial if and only if

  1. $\displaystyle B_{0}(y)=1$;
  2. $\displaystyle B_{k}^{\prime}(y)=k B_{k-1}(y)$;
  3. $\displaystyle\int_{0}^{1} B_{k}(y) \,\mathrm d y=0$ for $k \geqslant 1$.

Are these polynomials and those we found with the generating function $\dfrac{xe^{xy}}{e^x-1}$ exactly the same? They are. To prove this, we first demonstrate this recursive sequence of polynomials is unique. Then, we prove our original definition of the Bernoulli polynomials satisfies these three properties. Therefore, the two definitions are equivalent.

There are several other properties of the Bernoulli polynomials

  1. $B_{k}(1)=(-1)^{k} B_{k}$
  2. $B_{2 k}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)=-\frac{1}{2}\left(1-3^{1-2 k}\right) B_{2 k}$
  3. $B_{2 k}\left(\frac{1}{6}\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left(1-2^{1-2 k}\right)\left(1-3^{1-2 k}\right) B_{2 k}$
  4. $B_{k}(y)=(B+y)^{k},$ where $B^{k}$ is interpreted as the Bernoulli number $B_{k}$ (much like the mnemonic for the Bernoulli numbers themselves)
  5. $B_{k}=(B-y)^{k},$ where $B_{k}$ is interpreted as the Bernoulli polynomial $B_{k}(y)$.
